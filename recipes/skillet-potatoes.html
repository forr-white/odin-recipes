Crispy Skillet Potatoes

Developed by Cook Anything Kitchen

sides • American

There is a fine line between soggy, greasy potatoes and the crispy, golden perfection you get at a good diner. The secret lies in three things: using starchy Russet potatoes, removing excess moisture before cooking, and using a heavy cast iron skillet that holds heat evenly.

Recommended Tools

  • Cast Iron Skillet (10-12 inch) (Essential for the best crust)
  • Fish Spatula (Thin metal edge helps get under the potatoes without breaking the crust)
  • Paper Towels or clean dish cloth

Ingredients

  • 2 large Russet potatoes, scrubbed and cleaned (peeling is optional)
  • ½ cup neutral oil with a high smoke point (Avocado, Vegetable, or Canola)
  • Salt (to taste)
  • Freshly cracked black pepper (optional)

Directions

  1. Prep & Dry (Crucial): Cut the potatoes into ½-inch cubes. Do not rinse them. Instead, use a clean kitchen towel or paper towels to pat the potato pieces completely dry. Any surface moisture will cause steam and prevent browning.
  2. Heat Oil: Heat a 10–12 inch cast iron skillet over medium heat. Add the oil. Let the pan heat up for 3–5 minutes. Test: Place one potato cube in; if it sizzles immediately, the oil is ready.
  3. Cook in Batches: Add a single layer of potatoes to the skillet. Do not overcrowd the pan. If the potatoes are touching or piled up, they will steam instead of fry. You will likely need to do 3–4 batches.
  4. The "Don't Touch" Phase: Cook for about 7 minutes on the first side without moving them. Peeking ruins the crust!
  5. Flip: Flip the potatoes and cook for another 3–4 minutes until golden brown on all sides and tender in the middle.
  6. Drain & Season: Remove the crispy potatoes to a paper towel-lined plate to drain excess oil. Immediately hit them with a generous pinch of salt while they are hot. Repeat with remaining batches.

Notes

  • No Lid: Never cover the pan with a lid. A lid traps moisture, which turns your crispy fries into soggy boiled potatoes.
  • Seasoning: Add dried herbs (like rosemary or thyme) or black pepper after cooking. If you add them at the beginning, they will burn in the hot oil.
  • Russet vs. Yukon: Russets are high-starch and low-moisture, making them the fluffiest and crispiest choice. Waxy potatoes (like Red or Yukon) will be creamier but less crispy.
