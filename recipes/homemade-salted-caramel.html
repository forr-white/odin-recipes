Homemade Salted Caramel

Developed by Forrest White & Cook Anything Kitchen

desserts • American

Making homemade caramel is a test of patience, but the reward is a candy that melts in your mouth with a flavor that store-bought versions can't touch. This recipe uses evaporated milk for deep richness and corn syrup to prevent crystallization. This creates a "soft-ball" caramel that is chewy but won't break your teeth.

  • Candy Thermometer (Mandatory: You cannot guess the temperature with caramel)
  • Heavy-Bottomed Stock Pot (Prevents the sugar from scorching)
  • Parchment Paper (For lining the pan and wrapping individual pieces)

Ingredients

  • 2 sticks (1 cup) unsalted butter
  • 4 cups white granulated sugar
  • 2 cups white corn syrup
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 2 cans (12 oz each) evaporated milk
  • Flaky sea salt (for topping)

Directions

  1. The Base: In a large, heavy-bottomed pot, combine the butter, sugar, corn syrup, and salt. Bring the mixture to a boil over medium heat.
  2. The Slow Pour: This is the most important step. While stirring constantly, slowly pour in the evaporated milk. Aim to take about 10 minutes per can. Pouring too fast will drop the temperature too quickly and can cause the mixture to separate.
  3. The Boil: Once all the milk is incorporated, the mixture will be a pale cream color. Continue stirring over medium heat as it deepens into a golden brown.
  4. Temperature Check: Attach your candy thermometer. Continue cooking and stirring until the mixture reaches 235°F–238°F (113°C–114°C). This is the "soft-ball" stage.
  5. Set: Immediately remove from heat and pour the hot caramel into a 9x13-inch pan lined with parchment paper. Do not scrape the very bottom of the pot (to avoid any burnt bits).
  6. Salt & Cool: Let the caramel sit for 10 minutes, then sprinkle generously with flaky sea salt. Allow to cool completely at room temperature, then refrigerate for 30 minutes to firm up for cutting.
  7. Cut & Wrap: Lift the parchment paper out of the pan. Use a sharp, slightly oiled knife to cut the caramel into ¾" x 1" pieces. Wrap each piece tightly in a small square of parchment paper to prevent sticking.

Notes

  • Pot Selection: Use a pot much larger than you think you need. The caramel bubbles up significantly when the milk is added and can overflow easily.
  • The "Cold Water" Test: If you don't trust your thermometer, drop a small spoonful of the hot syrup into a bowl of very cold water. If it forms a ball that stays together but is still soft and pliable when flattened, it's ready.
  • Firmness: If you prefer a firmer, harder caramel, cook it to 242°F–245°F.
  • Storage: Store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 weeks. If your kitchen is very warm, keep them in the fridge.
