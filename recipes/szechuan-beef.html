Szechuan Beef Stir-Fry

Developed by Cook Anything Kitchen

dinner • Chinese

This recipe uses a classic Chinese restaurant technique called velveting—coating the beef in egg whites and cornstarch before frying. This seals in moisture and gives the meat that silky, tender texture you love. Packed with crunchy vegetables like celery, carrots, and peppers, this is a complete meal in one wok.

Ingredients

The Beef & Marinade (Velveting)

  • 1 lb beef skirt steak, sliced thinly against the grain
  • 2 egg whites
  • 6 tbsp cornstarch
  • 2 tbsp oil (vegetable or peanut oil preferred)
  • 2 tsp sesame seed oil
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 2 pinches black pepper

The Stir-Fry Vegetables

  • 4 tbsp oil (for frying)
  • 2 tsp minced ginger
  • 2 tsp minced garlic
  • 1½ cups sliced onion
  • 1½ cups sliced green bell pepper
  • 2 cups sliced mushrooms
  • 2 cups chopped celery
  • 1 cup chopped carrots

The Sauce & Finishing

  • 2 tbsp Chinese cooking wine (Shaoxing wine) or dry sherry
  • 1 cup water
  • Thickener: 1 tbsp cornstarch mixed with 2 tbsp water

Directions

  1. Marinate (Velvet the Beef): In a bowl, combine the sliced beef, salt, sesame oil, black pepper, egg whites, 6 tablespoons of cornstarch, and 2 tablespoons of oil. Mix well by hand until the beef is evenly coated and sticky. Let it marinate for 15–20 minutes.
  2. Sear Beef: Heat 4 tablespoons of oil in a large wok or skillet over medium-high heat. Add the marinated beef in a single layer (do this in batches if needed). Stir-fry until golden brown and cooked through. Remove the beef from the pan and set aside.
  3. Aromatics & Veggies: In the same wok (add a splash more oil if dry), add the minced ginger and garlic. Stir-fry for 10 seconds until fragrant. Add the onion, carrots, green pepper, celery, and mushrooms. Cook for 3–5 minutes, stirring frequently, until the vegetables begin to soften but still have crunch.
  4. Make Sauce: Add the cooking wine and 1 cup of water to the vegetables. Scrape up any browned bits from the bottom of the wok. Return the cooked beef to the pan and toss everything together.
  5. Thicken: In a small bowl, whisk the remaining 1 tablespoon cornstarch with 2 tablespoons water to create a slurry. Slowly pour this into the boiling sauce while stirring. Cook for 1 minute until the sauce thickens and becomes glossy.
  6. Serve: Serve hot over steamed rice or noodles.

Notes

  • Where's the Heat? Traditional Szechuan beef is spicy. This version is mild and vegetable-forward. To make it spicy, add 1 tablespoon of Szechuan peppercorns or 6–8 dried red chilies during Step 3 with the ginger and garlic.
  • Oil Choice: The recipe calls for olive oil, but for high-heat wok cooking, vegetable, canola, or peanut oil is much better as it won't burn or smoke as easily.
  • Sodium: If the sauce tastes bland, add 1–2 tablespoons of soy sauce in Step 4.
