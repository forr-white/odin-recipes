Chicken and Dumplings

Developed by Forrest White & Cook Anything Kitchen

soups • Southern American

Chicken and Dumplings is the ultimate Southern comfort food. We skip the flat, chewy noodle-style dumplings in favor of fluffy "drop" dumplings that steam right on top of the rich, creamy stew. Using evaporated milk gives the broth a velvety texture without being overly heavy.

Ingredients

For the Soup Base

  • 6 tablespoons butter
  • 1 cup diced yellow onion
  • 1 cup matchstick carrots
  • 1 cup diced celery
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 3 tablespoons all-purpose flour
  • 1 (12 oz) can evaporated milk (or half and half)
  • 1 (32 oz) quart chicken stock
  • 4 cups shredded cooked chicken (rotisserie works great)
  • 1 tablespoon fresh thyme (or 1 teaspoon dried thyme), optional
  • 2 teaspoons freshly cracked black pepper, or to taste
  • Salt, to taste

For the Dumplings

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour
  • 1 tablespoon + 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon freshly cracked black pepper
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 tablespoon fresh thyme (or 1 teaspoon dried thyme), optional
  • ¾ cup (6 oz) whole milk
  • 4 tablespoons butter, melted

Directions

Make the Soup Base

  1. Sauté Veggies: In a large heavy-bottomed pot like a Dutch oven, melt butter over medium-high heat. Add onion, carrots, and celery. Cook for 5 minutes, until vegetables begin to soften. Add garlic and cook for 1 minute more.
  2. Make Roux: Add flour and stir to combine. Cook for 1 minute, stirring constantly to cook out the raw flour taste. Slowly add the evaporated milk and chicken stock, stirring constantly to prevent lumps.
  3. Simmer: Bring to a boil and add the chicken, thyme, black pepper, and salt. Lower the heat and let the soup simmer, uncovered, while you make the dumplings.

Make and Cook Dumplings

  1. Mix Dry: In a medium-sized bowl, whisk to combine the flour, baking powder, pepper, salt, and thyme.
  2. Add Wet: Make a well in the center of the flour mixture and pour the milk and melted butter into the center. Using a wooden spoon (or rubber spatula), stir together until a dough ball forms. If your dough seems too dry, you can add an extra tablespoon of milk. Do not overmix.
  3. Scoop: Using a medium-sized cookie scoop, scoop dough and drop directly into the simmering soup. Place them around the pot, leaving space between them if possible. Once all dumplings have been added, gently press them down so the soup runs over just the tops of them.
  4. Steam: Place the lid on your pot and lower the heat to a low simmer. You don’t want the bottom of the soup to burn, but you need a gentle bubble to steam the dumplings.
  5. Cook: Cook dumplings for 15 minutes with the pot lid on. Do not lift the lid frequently! About halfway through, you can gently stir to ensure they aren't sticking together, but keep the lid on as much as possible to trap the steam.
  6. Serve: Cut one dumpling in half to ensure it is cooked through (it should look like fluffy bread inside, not doughy). Serve hot!

Notes

  • Don't Peek: The dumplings cook by steaming. Lifting the lid lets the heat escape and can result in dense, undercooked dumplings.
  • Rotisserie Chicken: Using a store-bought rotisserie chicken saves a ton of time and adds great flavor.
  • Thickness: The flour on the outside of the dumplings will naturally thicken the soup as they cook. If the soup is too thick for your liking at the end, stir in a splash of water or broth.
  • Storage: This soup reheats well, though the dumplings may absorb more liquid overnight. You may need to add a little broth when reheating leftovers.
