Creamy Green Salsa

Developed by Forrest White & Cook Anything Kitchen

sauce • Mexican

If you have ever wondered why the green salsa at your favorite taco stand is so creamy but doesn't taste exactly like avocado, this is the secret. It is actually an emulsion of oil and jalapeños. Often called "Salsa Doña" or "Fake Guacamole," it is spicy, savory, and perfect for drizzling over street tacos.

  • High-Powered Blender (Essential for emulsifying the oil into a creamy sauce)
  • Deep Skillet or Saucepan (For poaching the peppers in oil)
  • Gloves (Highly recommended when handling this many jalapeños)

Ingredients

  • 10 jalapeños, deseeded and deveined (leave seeds in 1 or 2 for extra heat)
  • ½ white onion, cut into large chunks
  • 3–4 garlic cloves, peeled
  • ¾ to 1 cup vegetable oil (or mild olive oil)
  • Salt to taste

Directions

  1. Poach Peppers: Pour the oil into a skillet or saucepan and heat on low-to-medium heat. Place the cleaned jalapeños in the oil. Simmer gently for 15–20 minutes. You want the peppers to turn a pale, dull green color and become soft. Do not fry them to a crisp; you are essentially poaching them.
  2. Add Aromatics: Add the onion and garlic cloves to the oil. Simmer for about 5 more minutes, until the onion is translucent and the garlic is fragrant but not burnt.
  3. Cool: Remove from heat and cover. Let it sit for at least 30 minutes. This allows the flavors to meld, but more importantly, blending hot oil is dangerous. The oil must be warm or room temperature, not boiling hot.
  4. Blend: Transfer everything to a blender—the peppers, onions, garlic, and all of the oil.
  5. Emulsify: Add salt to your liking. Blend on high until completely smooth. The mixture will change color from dark oily green to a creamy, pale opaque green (like guacamole).
  6. Serve: Taste and add more salt if needed. Serve immediately or store in the fridge.

Notes

  • No Avocado: There is absolutely no avocado in this recipe. The creamy texture comes entirely from emulsifying the oil with the fiber of the peppers.
  • Oil Choice: Vegetable, Canola, or Corn oil works best here for a neutral flavor. Extra Virgin Olive Oil can turn bitter when blended at high speeds.
  • Heat Level: This salsa is naturally spicy. To tame it, be very diligent about removing all seeds and veins. To make it hotter, leave the seeds in half of the peppers.
  • Storage: Because it is oil-based and cooked, this salsa lasts much longer than avocado salsa. It keeps well in the fridge for 1–2 weeks.
