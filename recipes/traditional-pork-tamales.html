Traditional Pork Tamales

Developed by Cook Anything Kitchen

dinner • Mexican

Making tamales is rarely a solo activity; it is a celebration known as a Tamalada. While the process is long, the result is the ultimate comfort food: savory shredded pork coated in a rich red chili sauce, encased in light, fluffy corn masa. This recipe makes a large batch (about 50), so invite some friends over to help spread the masa!

Ingredients

The Pork Filling

  • 3½ lbs pork shoulder (pork butt), cut into large chunks
  • 10 cups water
  • 1 medium onion, quartered
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1½ teaspoons salt
  • 4 cups Red Chili Sauce (prepared ahead)

The Masa Dough

  • ¾ cup vegetable shortening (or lard for traditional flavor)
  • 6 cups masa harina (corn flour for tamales)
  • 2 teaspoons baking powder
  • 2 teaspoons salt
  • 6 cups reserved pork broth (from cooking the meat)

Wrappers

  • 50 dried corn husks (approx. 8 inches long)

Directions

1. Prepare the Meat

  1. Boil: In a 5-quart Dutch oven or large pot, combine the pork, water, onion, garlic, and 1½ teaspoons salt. Bring to a boil.
  2. Simmer: Reduce heat, cover, and simmer for about 2½ hours, or until the meat is falling-apart tender.
  3. Shred: Remove meat from broth. Strain and save the broth (you need 6 cups for the masa). Shred the meat using two forks, discarding excess fat.
  4. Sauce: In a large saucepan, heat the Red Chili Sauce. Add the shredded pork and simmer covered for 10 minutes to infuse the flavor. Remove from heat.

2. Prepare the Masa

  1. Soak Husks: While meat cools, soak corn husks in a sink or bowl of warm water for at least 20 minutes to soften. Rinse off any corn silk and drain well.
  2. Whip Fat: In a large bowl (or stand mixer), beat the shortening (or lard) on medium speed for 1 minute until fluffy and white.
  3. Mix Dry: In a separate bowl, stir together masa harina, baking powder, and 2 teaspoons salt.
  4. Combine: Alternately add the masa mixture and the warm reserved pork broth to the whipped shortening. Beat well after each addition. Continue beating until the dough is soft, spreadable, and slightly sticky (like thick hummus). (See Notes for the "Float Test").

3. Assemble & Steam

  1. Spread: Take a corn husk. Spread about 2 tablespoons of masa into a square in the center-bottom half of the husk (smooth side up), leaving space at the top and bottom.
  2. Fill: Place about 1 tablespoon of the pork mixture down the center of the masa.
  3. Fold: Fold the sides of the husk in so the masa wraps around the meat. Fold the pointy bottom tip up to close the bottom. Leave the top open.
  4. Stack: Prepare a steamer pot. Place a ball of foil or extra husks in the center of the steamer rack to help lean the tamales against. Arrange tamales standing upright (open side up).
  5. Steam: Add water to the pot (just below the rack level). Cover tightly. Steam for 40–50 minutes.
  6. Check: To test doneness, remove one tamale and let it cool for a minute. If the husk peels away cleanly from the masa without sticking, they are done. If it sticks, steam for 10 more minutes.

Notes

  • The Float Test: To ensure your tamales are fluffy and not dense, take a tiny piece of your prepared masa and drop it into a cup of cold water. If it floats, it is ready. If it sinks, you need to whip more air into it (or add a little more melted fat/broth).
  • Shortening vs. Lard: While shortening works well, traditional tamales use lard (Manteca). Lard provides a richer flavor and incredible texture.
  • Freezing: Tamales freeze perfectly. Let them cool completely, then bag them in Ziplocs. Steam them frozen for 20 minutes to reheat.
  • Don't Burn the Pot: When steaming, keep a kettle of hot water nearby. Check the steamer occasionally to ensure the water hasn't boiled away, or you will burn the pot and smoke the tamales.
