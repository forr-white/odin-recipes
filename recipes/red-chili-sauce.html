Red Chili Sauce

Developed by Cook Anything Kitchen

sauce • Mexican

This is the real deal. While store-bought enchilada sauce is often made from tomato paste and chili powder, authentic red sauce starts with whole dried chile pods. The result is an earthy, complex, and velvety sauce that serves as the backbone for enchiladas, tamales, and "wet" burritos.

Ingredients

  • 15 large dried chilies (California, New Mexico, or Guajillo for mild/medium; Arbol for hot)
  • 4–5 garlic cloves, peeled
  • 2 teaspoons ground cumin
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 2 teaspoons all-purpose flour
  • 2 teaspoons olive oil or melted shortening/lard
  • 2½ cups soaking water (reserved from rehydrating peppers)

Directions

  1. Clean Peppers: Remove the stems from the dried chili peppers. Shake out or slice open to remove as many seeds as possible (seeds add bitterness, not just heat).
  2. Toast: Preheat oven to 350°F (175°C). Place peppers in a single layer on a baking sheet. Roast for 2–5 minutes. Watch closely! You want a sweet, roasted aroma. If they turn black, they will taste bitter and must be discarded.
  3. Soak: Remove from oven and place in a bowl. Cover with boiling hot water. Let soak for 30 minutes until soft and pliable.
  4. Blend: Remove peppers from the water and place them in a blender. Add 2½ cups of the soaking water (discard the rest or save for thinning later), the garlic cloves, cumin, and salt. Cover and blend on high until perfectly smooth.
  5. The Roux: In a 2-quart saucepan, heat the oil (or shortening) over medium heat. Stir in the flour and cook for 1 minute until bubbly and slightly browned.
  6. Combine & Simmer: Pour the blended chili mixture into the saucepan with the roux. (Tip: If your blender didn't get the skins completely smooth, pour the mixture through a wire mesh strainer into the pan).
  7. Finish: Simmer uncovered for 5–10 minutes until slightly thickened. Taste and add more salt if needed.

Notes

  • Chili Varieties: Guajillo peppers provide a bright red color and tangy flavor. Ancho peppers provide a dark color and raisin-like sweetness. A mix of both is often the best choice.
  • Bitter Sauce? If the sauce tastes slightly bitter (usually from the skins or toasting), stir in a pinch of sugar or a teaspoon of tomato paste to balance it out.
  • Straining: Unless you have a professional-grade blender, bits of the tough pepper skins will remain. Straining the sauce through a sieve creates that restaurant-quality velvety texture.
