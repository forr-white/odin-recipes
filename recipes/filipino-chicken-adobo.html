Filipino Chicken Adobo

Developed by Forrest White & Cook Anything Kitchen

dinner • Filipino

Adobo is often considered the national dish of the Philippines. It is a cooking method where meat is braised in a mixture of soy sauce, vinegar, garlic, and peppercorns. The result is a perfect balance of salty, sour, and savory flavors that reduces down into a sticky, rich glaze. It is best served over steaming white rice to soak up the sauce.

Ingredients

Chicken and Marinade

  • 1.5 lb (750g) chicken thigh fillets, boneless and skinless (about 5–6 pieces)
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • ⅓ cup (85ml) soy sauce (ordinary all-purpose or light; do not use dark soy sauce)
  • ⅓ cup + 2 tbsp white vinegar
  • 4 fresh bay leaves (or 3 dried)

For Cooking

  • 2 tbsp vegetable, canola, or peanut oil (separated)
  • 3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1 small brown onion, diced
  • 1½ cups (375 ml) water
  • 2 tbsp brown sugar
  • 1 tbsp whole black peppercorns (or 2 tsp coarse cracked pepper)

Serving

  • 2 green onions/scallions, sliced
  • Steamed white rice

Directions

  1. Marinate: Combine the chicken and marinade ingredients in a bowl. Mix well. Marinate for at least 20 minutes (or overnight for deeper flavor).
  2. Sear: Heat 1 tablespoon of oil in a skillet over high heat. Remove chicken from the marinade (reserve the marinade—do not throw it away!) and place in the pan. Sear both sides until browned, about 1 minute per side. The chicken should not be cooked through yet. Remove chicken from the skillet and set aside.
  3. Aromatics: Heat the remaining oil in the same skillet. Add the garlic and onion; cook for 1½ minutes until fragrant.
  4. Make Sauce: Pour in the reserved marinade, water, brown sugar, and black peppercorns. Bring to a simmer, then turn heat down to medium-high. Simmer for 5 minutes. (Note: Do not stir vigorously right away; let the vinegar "cook off" slightly).
  5. Braise: Return the chicken to the pan, smooth side down. Simmer uncovered for 20 to 25 minutes. Turn the chicken halfway through (around the 15-minute mark). You want the sauce to reduce down to a thick, jam-like syrup.
  6. Glaze: If the chicken is tender but the sauce is still too watery, remove the chicken onto a plate temporarily. Let the sauce boil vigorously by itself until it thickens, then return the chicken to the skillet to coat in the glaze.
  7. Serve: Garnish with sliced green onions and serve hot over white rice.

Notes

  • Vinegar Rule: A common Filipino cooking rule for adobo is not to stir the pot immediately after adding the vinegar. Letting it simmer undisturbed helps mellow the harsh acid taste.
  • Soy Sauce: Use standard soy sauce (like Kikkoman or Silver Swan). "Dark" soy sauce is too thick and salty for the main amount, though a splash can be added for color.
  • Peppercorns: Whole peppercorns are traditional, but if you dislike biting into them, you can crush them slightly or use coarse ground pepper.
