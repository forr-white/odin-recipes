Cajun Hollandaise

Developed by Forrest White & Cook Anything Kitchen

sauce • Cajun French Fusion

Give your brunch a Southern kick with this Cajun Hollandaise. It takes the rich, buttery texture of the classic French sauce and infuses it with bold spices, hot sauce, and a touch of smoke. It is the ultimate topping for Crab Cake Benedict, grilled fish, or even roasted asparagus.

  • Heatproof Glass Bowl (essential for the double boiler method)
  • Whisk (constant whisking is the key to preventing scrambled eggs)

Ingredients

  • 3 large egg yolks
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice
  • ½ cup (1 stick) unsalted butter, melted and warm
  • 1–1½ tsp Cajun seasoning (homemade or store-bought; adjust to heat level)
  • ¼ tsp smoked paprika
  • ¼ tsp garlic powder
  • Pinch cayenne pepper (optional for extra heat)
  • ¼ tsp salt (adjust depending on seasoning blend)
  • 2–3 tsp hot sauce of choice (Crystal or Louisiana recommended)
  • Optional: squeeze of orange juice for a subtle Creole vibe

Directions

  1. Prepare the Double Boiler: Bring 1–2 inches of water to a gentle simmer in a small pot.
  2. Whisk the Yolks: In a heatproof bowl that fits over the pot, whisk egg yolks and lemon juice until slightly thickened.
  3. Cook over Steam: Place the bowl over (not in) the simmering water. Continue whisking constantly so the eggs don’t scramble.
  4. Emulsify: While whisking vigorously, slowly stream in the warm melted butter a little at a time until the sauce is thick, glossy, and fully emulsified.
  5. Season: Remove from heat. Whisk in the Cajun seasoning, smoked paprika, garlic powder, cayenne (if using), salt, and hot sauce. Adjust to taste.
  6. Serve: Hollandaise is best fresh. Serve immediately or keep warm (not hot) over a thermos or warm water bath.

Notes

  • Creole Variation: For a true Creole-style hollandaise, swap the Cajun seasoning for Creole seasoning, which has more herbs (like oregano, thyme, and basil) and slightly less heat.
  • Add Tang: Add a splash of Worcestershire sauce and a tiny pinch of Creole mustard to create the classic tang found in Creole sauces.
  • Citrus Twist: Finish the sauce with a small squeeze of orange juice instead of lemon for a brighter, more citrus-forward flavor profile.
  • Broken Sauce? If the sauce separates, whisk a teaspoon of boiling water into it vigorously to bring it back together.
