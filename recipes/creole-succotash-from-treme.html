Creole Succotash from Treme

Adapted from Treme / New Orleans Tradition

dinner • Creole

If you watched the HBO series Treme, you might recognize this dish. Unlike the dry corn-and-bean side dish many people know as succotash, the New Orleans version is a rich, complex stew. Loaded with the "Holy Trinity" of vegetables, smoky pork, and fresh seafood, it sits somewhere between a gumbo and a vegetable stew. It takes time, but the depth of flavor is unforgettable.

  • Wooden Spoon (For scraping the flavorful fond from the bottom of the pot)

Ingredients

  • 2 tablespoons unsalted butter
  • 1 (28 oz) can crushed tomatoes
  • 1 yellow onion, chopped
  • 1 green bell pepper, seeded and chopped
  • 2 ribs celery, chopped
  • 1 pound fresh or frozen okra, trimmed and cut into 1-inch pieces
  • 1 pound fresh or frozen lima beans
  • 1 pound fresh or frozen corn kernels
  • 1 pound smoked pork sausage (Andouille is best), cut into ¾-inch slices
  • 1 pound boneless smoked ham, cut into 1-inch cubes
  • 2 bay leaves
  • Pinch of dried thyme
  • ½ to 1 cup water (reserved, if needed)
  • 1 pound medium shrimp, peeled and deveined
  • Hot cooked rice for serving

Directions

  1. The Trinity: In a large heavy-bottomed saucepan or Dutch oven over medium heat, melt the butter. Add the crushed tomatoes, onion, bell pepper, and celery. Stir well to coat.
  2. Sauté: Cook uncovered, stirring occasionally, until the vegetables are tender, about 10 to 15 minutes.
  3. The Big Dump: Stir in the okra, lima beans, corn, smoked sausage, ham, bay leaves, and thyme.
  4. Simmer: Cook, still uncovered, until the vegetables are soft and the flavors have melded, about 1 hour. Stir occasionally to prevent sticking.
  5. Adjust Liquid: If the mixture seems too dry toward the end of cooking, stir in the reserved water a little at a time to moisten. Then, cover the pan to trap the steam.
  6. Shrimp: Reduce the heat to low. Stir in the raw shrimp. Cook, stirring often, until the shrimp turn pink and opaque (about 10–15 minutes). Do not overcook.
  7. Serve: Remove from heat and discard the bay leaves. Mound rice on large plates or bowls, spoon the generous succotash stew over the rice, and serve.

Notes

  • The Holy Trinity: This recipe relies on the classic Cajun/Creole base of onions, bell peppers, and celery. Do not skip or skimp on these.
  • Okra Texture: Cooking the okra for a long period in the acidic tomato base helps mitigate the "slime" texture often associated with it, resulting in a natural thickener for the stew.
  • Yield: This is a large-batch recipe designed to feed a crowd. It freezes exceptionally well (though the shrimp texture may change slightly upon reheating).
