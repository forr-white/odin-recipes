Focaccia Bread

Developed by Forrest White & Cook Anything Kitchen

bread • Italian

Focaccia is one of the easiest and most forgiving breads to make at home. It is known for its high hydration dough, which creates an incredibly airy, bubbly interior and a crispy, olive-oil-fried crust. The signature dimpling step isn't just for looks—it creates little pockets for the olive oil and salt to pool in.

Recommended Tools

  • Stand Mixer (This dough is very sticky and hard to knead by hand)
  • 9x13 Baking Pan (Metal pans yield a crispier bottom than glass)
  • Silicone Spatula (For scraping the sticky dough out of the bowl)

Ingredients

  • 1¾ cups warm water (105° to 115°F)
  • 2 teaspoons cane sugar (or honey)
  • 1 packet (¼-oz) active dry yeast (2¼ tsp)
  • 500 grams all-purpose flour (approx. 4 cups, spooned and leveled)
  • 2 teaspoons sea salt
  • 6 tablespoons extra-virgin olive oil, divided, plus more for your hands
  • Flaky sea salt (Maldon), for sprinkling
  • Chopped fresh rosemary, for sprinkling (optional)

Directions

  1. Activate Yeast: In the bowl of a stand mixer fitted with a dough hook, stir together the warm water and sugar. Sprinkle the yeast on top and stir again. Set aside for 5 minutes until foamy. (If it doesn't foam, your yeast is dead; start over).
  2. Mix Dough: Add the flour and salt. Mix on low speed until a shaggy dough forms. Increase speed to medium and mix for 5 minutes. The dough will be very elastic and sticky, beginning to pull away from the sides of the bowl.
  3. First Rise: Brush a large bowl with 2 tablespoons of olive oil. Transfer the dough to the bowl using a spatula (it will be too sticky to handle). Lightly oil the top of the dough. Cover with plastic wrap or a damp towel and let rise until doubled in size, 1 to 1½ hours.
  4. Shape: Brush a 9x13-inch baking dish with 2 more tablespoons of olive oil. Uncover the dough. With oiled hands, gently deflate the dough by folding the edges into the center. Transfer the dough to the prepared baking pan.
  5. Second Rise: Press the dough toward the edges of the pan. If it springs back, let it rest for 10 minutes then try again. Cover and let rise until puffy and doubled, about 45 minutes. (Preheat oven to 425°F/220°C during the last 30 minutes of this rise).
  6. Dimple & Bake: Remove the cover. Drizzle the remaining 2 tablespoons of olive oil over the dough. With oiled fingers, press straight down to create deep dimples all over the surface (touch the bottom of the pan). Sprinkle with flaky salt and rosemary. Bake for 20–30 minutes until golden brown.

Notes

  • Sticky Dough: Do not be tempted to add more flour. High hydration is key to the big air bubbles. Keep your hands oiled to prevent sticking.
  • Pan Choice: A metal baking pan conducts heat better and will give you a crunchier bottom crust than a glass dish.
  • Toppings: Beyond rosemary, try topping with cherry tomatoes, olives, caramelized onions, or even thinly sliced potatoes.
