Sausage and Sauerkraut Skillet with Potatoes

Developed by Cook Anything Kitchen

dinner • German

This is essentially a deconstructed German feast made in a single pan. It combines the savory richness of pork sausage with the sharp tang of sauerkraut and the earthiness of red potatoes. The secret ingredient is the apple—its natural sweetness creates a perfect balance against the acidity of the kraut.

Ingredients

The Meat & Fat

  • 1 lb bratwurst or smoked sausage (see notes on raw vs. cooked)
  • 2 tablespoons lard, bacon grease, or coconut oil

Vegetables & Fruit

  • 5 medium red potatoes, cut into small ½-inch cubes
  • 1 medium onion, diced
  • 1 medium apple (Gala or Fuji), diced
  • 16 oz sauerkraut (homemade or store-bought), drained

Seasoning

  • ½ teaspoon salt
  • ½ teaspoon black pepper
  • ¼ teaspoon crushed red pepper flakes (optional)

Directions

  1. Prep Sausage: If using raw bratwurst, remove the casings and brown the meat in the skillet first. Remove and set aside. If using smoked/precooked sausage, slice into ¼-inch coins and proceed to step 2.
  2. Brown Potatoes: Heat the lard or oil over medium-high heat in a large skillet. Add the cubed potatoes. Cook, stirring occasionally, until they are browned and starting to soften (about 8–10 minutes). Tip: Cut potatoes small so they cook through quickly.
  3. Aromatics: Add the diced apples and onions to the skillet with the potatoes. Cook for another 3–4 minutes until the onions are translucent.
  4. Simmer: Add the sauerkraut and the sausage (cooked or sliced). Stir to combine. Reduce heat to Low. Cover with a lid and simmer for 10 minutes. This steams the potatoes until fully tender and melds the flavors.
  5. Finish: Remove the lid. Season with salt, black pepper, and red pepper flakes. Serve hot directly from the skillet.

Notes

  • Raw vs. Pre-Cooked: If you buy raw bratwurst links, you must cook them fully before slicing, or crumble them out of the casing like ground meat. Pre-cooked smoked sausage (like Kielbasa) just needs to be warmed through.
  • Drain the Kraut: Make sure to drain most of the brine from the sauerkraut so the skillet doesn't become too watery. If you love sour flavors, save a splash of the juice to add at the end.
  • Variation: For an even more authentic German flavor, add ½ teaspoon of caraway seeds when you add the sauerkraut.
